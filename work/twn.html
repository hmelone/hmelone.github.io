Case Study / 04 · 2015 — 2022

Standing up a knowledge system at TWN.

Building an end-to-end knowledge, training, and quality ecosystem from scratch, and turning it into the operational backbone of a growing service organization.

Role
Knowledge, Training & Quality Manager
Built at
TWN Communications
Audience
Internal — Support, Field, Ops
Disciplines
Knowledge systems · Training · QA
01 / Context

No system, just stories.

When I took on this role, the company didn't have a knowledge program. It had a folder structure, a few SOP documents in inconsistent formats, and a team of agents who (impressively) held an enormous amount of operational knowledge in their heads. Onboarding a new hire took weeks of shadowing. Help desk inquiries piled up because the answer existed somewhere but couldn't be found at the moment of need.

The mandate was broad and it was foundational: build the knowledge, training, and quality ecosystem from the ground up, and make it the single source of truth across functions. Not a documentation library. An operating system for the work itself.

02 / Approach

One system, three disciplines, one source of truth.

I had the unusual advantage of owning knowledge, training, and quality as a combined function, which meant I could design them as one system instead of three competing ones. That single ownership shaped every decision.

  1. Designed the knowledge base as the single source of truth.

    Stood up a company-wide knowledge base and SOP system with consistent structure, taxonomy, and lifecycle, the canonical answer to any operational question. No more "ask the senior agent." The system became the senior agent.

  2. Built training as a layer on top of the knowledge base.

    Onboarding programs, eLearning modules, and multimedia documentation aligned to the same content the team would use after onboarding, so what new hires learned in week one was the same material they'd reach for in month six.

  3. Established QA as a feedback loop, not a hammer.

    Quality frameworks for content and support interactions surfaced where the knowledge base was wrong, missing, or hard to use. QA findings became inputs to content improvement, turning quality from a punitive function into a continuous improvement engine.

  4. Led the cross-functional org.

    Managed teams across knowledge, technical writing, training, software QA, and call-center quality, setting standards and priorities across functions and ensuring the system stayed coherent as the company scaled.

  5. Closed the loop with content analytics.

    Introduced analytics and feedback signals to identify recurring errors and content gaps, then drove targeted improvements in structure, accessibility, and accuracy. The system kept getting better instead of decaying.

03 / Outcomes

What changed.

30%

Reduction in time to onboard new hires after the system was in place.

55%

Reduction in help desk inquiries as self-service became reliable.

One
source of truth

A unified system replacing scattered SOPs, tribal knowledge, and undocumented process.

The deeper outcome was cultural: the team stopped relying on a few experts as bottlenecks. New hires ramped on the same material the senior agents used. Process changes propagated through the system instead of through hallway conversation. And the leadership team had a place to make operational decisions visible.

04 / Reflections

What this taught me.

Knowledge, training, and quality are one system. Splitting them across owners almost guarantees they'll drift. Owning all three in one role is what made the integration possible, and is part of why I think about content as operational infrastructure rather than as a documentation deliverable.

Single source of truth is a discipline, not a slogan. It only works if you actually retire the old places things used to live, and if leadership stops sending around a different version of the truth. Otherwise the system becomes one more place to check.

QA findings are content gold. Most organizations treat quality data as performance data. It's also the most direct signal about where your knowledge system is failing, and the cheapest input to making it better.
