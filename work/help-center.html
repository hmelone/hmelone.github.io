Designing a Help Center at the front of the post-sales ecosystem.

Architecting a dedicated help center as the trusted front door of a post-sales content ecosystem — separating authoritative help from community engagement so both can do their actual jobs.

Role
Strategy & design lead
Built at
A global SaaS company
Audience
Customers · Support · CS · AI
Disciplines
IA · Content strategy · UX · Governance
01 / Context

One front door, five different jobs.

All of the company’s post-sales content lived together on a single community-led site: knowledge articles, product documentation, training, release notes, peer discussion, thought leadership, events, downloads. It was a thoughtful structure for an earlier era. But as content scaled and customer expectations changed, the same site was being asked to serve five fundamentally different user goals at once: customers trying to fix a problem, customers seeking authoritative product guidance, customers wanting peer discussion, customers consuming training, and customers hunting for downloads.

Each of those goals demands a different UX pattern. Blended into one experience, they competed with each other, and the user lost the thread of which kind of content they were even looking at.

02 / The signal

The findability paradox.

On its surface, the data told two contradictory stories. Self-service content was deflecting over a million dollars in support costs in 2025 alone, clear, measurable ROI on the knowledge investment. But click-through rate on that same self-service content sat at around 2%, and the deflection trend was bending sharply downward through early 2026.

The instinct on a finding like that is to call it a findability problem, users can’t see the content. The data didn’t support that read. Visibility was fine. Users were clicking search results. The gap was in what they found when they got there: a flat surface where forum threads, promotional blogs, knowledge articles, and documentation all looked like equivalent answers, with no clear hierarchy of trust.

The real problem wasn’t findability. It was trust hierarchy. Users were clicking, not getting a definitive answer, and opening a case anyway.

03 / Thesis

Help and community do different jobs.

Knowledge and community are both essential, and they reinforce each other when designed well. They are not, however, the same kind of content, and treating them as interchangeable in a single experience makes both worse.

Knowledge is a system of record. Authoritative, lifecycle-managed, written for self-service success. The goal is resolution: how-to, troubleshooting, known issues, references. It needs to be governed, trusted, and instantly identifiable as official.

Community is engagement infrastructure. Peer-to-peer conversation, ideas, events, supplemental insight. The goal is discussion, not resolution. It needs to feel social, current, and clearly distinct from official guidance.

When the two are merged into a single search result, they undermine each other. Forum replies get treated as equivalent to documentation; users self-determine the hierarchy and often default to whatever looks fastest or most conversational; the official answer becomes harder to identify even when it's right there. The proposal: separate the two, design each for what it actually does, and connect them deliberately at the seams.

04 / Approach

Five structural moves.

  1. Launch a dedicated help center.

    A standalone help.[brand].com destination with task-based navigation (fix a problem, learn how, find known issues) and authoritative content as the primary surface. Clear trust signals separate official content from supplemental or peer-sourced material. The help center becomes the front door for customers in resolution mode.

  2. Restructure the information architecture around user intent.

    Replace internal-team-shaped navigation (Community, Knowledge, Training, Downloads, Support) with intent-shaped navigation (I have a problem, Is this a known issue?, What is the right way to do this?). Scope search by content type so knowledge articles, docs, and training compete only against equivalents, not against forum posts.

  3. Elevate community to its own home.

    Give community a dedicated URL, brand identity, and ownership model. Position it explicitly as peer engagement, ideas, and events, the place customers come to talk to other customers, not the place they come for an answer. Community managers own that space fully without competing for primacy with help content.

  4. Connect the two experiences at the seams.

    Smart cross-links surface community threads from the help center as supplemental, never as equivalent to authoritative content. Shared SSO and user profiles span both sites so the experience feels coherent. Case deflection routes from the help center into the support portal. The two destinations stay distinct but operate as one ecosystem.

  5. Make the help center the trusted source for AI.

    The same content layer feeds the in-product AI assistant, the support chatbot, and case-resolution tooling. By giving AI a single governed, authoritative source, rather than letting it draw from a blend of forum threads and promotional posts, the system produces answers customers and agents can rely on.

05 / The ecosystem

Four destinations, one coherent system.

The help center is the front door, but it doesn’t do everything alone. Documentation lives on its own dedicated site for users who need depth. Training lives on its own site for users who need to learn. Community lives on its own site for users who need peer input. Each has a clear job, a clear URL, and a clear UX pattern. The help center surfaces them contextually, never as equals to authoritative content, but at the moments where users need them.

06 / Status

Where this is now.

Research is complete. The strategic case is built and stakeholder buy-in is in place at the leadership level. The work is currently in cross-functional review with UX, product marketing, community, support, and customer success, aligning on scope, sequencing, and the boundaries between each destination.

This is a structural change to how an organization treats post-sales content as a whole, not a site redesign. The pace reflects that. I’ll update this case study with implementation outcomes as the work lands.

07 / Reflections

What this is teaching me.

Findability problems are usually trust problems in disguise. When users click and don’t resolve, the instinct is to surface more content or improve search. The real fix is often upstream: clarify what kind of content lives where, and what each kind is for. Once trust hierarchy is legible, findability follows.

Internal structure and user intent rarely match. Most help experiences reflect how the company organizes itself rather than how users think. Reorganizing around intent feels minor on a sitemap and is enormous in practice. It changes who owns what, how content is routed, and how teams measure success.

AI is forcing the post-sales content question whether teams are ready or not. Every chatbot, in-product assistant, and case-resolution tool needs a trustworthy source. Organizations that haven’t separated authoritative help from peer discussion are giving their AI tools a confused training set, and customers feel the result. The help center isn’t a site; it’s the content layer the rest of the system depends on.
