Case Study / 03 · 2024 — 2025

An AI authoring tool for knowledge articles.

Designing an internal tool that drafts and edits knowledge articles from support cases: on-brand, on-template, and grounded in real customer interactions.

Role
Designer & product owner
Built at
Flexera
Audience
Internal authors — Support & CS
Disciplines
AI workflows · Structured authoring · Style systems
01 / Context

Closing the gap between case and article.

Inside the broader Flexera knowledge transformation, one specific friction point kept surfacing: the gap between resolving a customer case and producing a publishable knowledge article from it. The information was there. The expertise was there. What was missing was time and structure, the cognitive overhead of turning a case resolution into a clean, standards-compliant article was high enough that valuable knowledge stayed locked in tickets.

By the time I started designing this tool, the system had what it needed to make AI-assisted authoring trustworthy: a defined taxonomy, structured content templates, an explicit style guide, and a governed lifecycle. The opportunity was to use AI to reduce the friction of capture, while letting the existing standards do the work of quality.

02 / Approach

Structure first, AI second.

The premise: AI is only useful in content workflows when it operates inside a structure. A model that writes "good-sounding" articles without enforced templates will reliably produce drift: off-brand voice, inconsistent metadata, hallucinated steps, or content that bypasses the lifecycle. The goal here was the opposite: every AI output had to land inside the same shape an experienced author would produce.

  1. Encoded the article templates as authoring constraints.

    The existing content types (how-to, troubleshooting, reference, known issue) became explicit scaffolds that the tool generates into. The model can't produce shape that doesn't exist in the IA, which removes a whole class of quality risk before review.

  2. Built the style guide into the prompt system.

    Voice, tense, terminology, accessibility conventions, formatting rules, the things a human editor would enforce on review, encoded as instructions the tool applies during drafting and again during edit. Style enforcement happens upstream of the human reviewer, not as a cleanup step.

  3. Grounded drafts in the actual case.

    The tool reads the support case, extracts symptoms, environment, root cause, and resolution, and structures them into the appropriate template, so the article reflects what really happened, not a plausible-sounding generalization.

  4. Designed for human-in-the-loop review.

    The tool is explicitly an assistant, not an autopilot. It produces a draft positioned for fast author review and approval, surfaces where it's uncertain, and integrates into the existing governance and quality workflow. The author stays accountable; the tool removes friction.

  5. Treated the tool as part of the knowledge system, not a side experiment.

    Outputs go through the same lifecycle, taxonomy, and quality reviews as any other article. There is no parallel “AI-generated content” channel with different rules, which is what keeps trust intact across customers and internal teams.

03 / Outcomes

What changed for authors.

The point of the tool isn't volume. It's conversion: turning resolved cases into reusable knowledge at a rate the system couldn't sustain through purely manual authoring.

Faster
case–to–publish

Less friction between resolving a case and publishing a reusable article.

Consistent
by default

Voice, structure, and metadata stay on-standard across every author.

Same
guardrails

AI output runs through the same governance, lifecycle, and quality reviews as human-authored content.

04 / Reflections

What I learned about AI & content.

The system is the prompt. Most of the quality of an AI-authored article comes from constraints set before the model is invoked: the template it must fill, the taxonomy it must label against, the style rules it must obey. Prompts that try to do all this at runtime are fragile.

Treat AI output as a draft, not a deliverable. The tool earns trust by being explicitly an assistant. Authors review, edit, and own. That framing changed how the program was received internally, less "AI is replacing us," more "AI is doing the boring half."

Invest in structure before you invest in models. Organizations rushing to put AI on top of unstructured knowledge bases are buying speed they can't trust. The tool worked because the underlying system was already governed.
