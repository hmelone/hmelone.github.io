The Flexera knowledge transformation.

Building a governed enterprise knowledge ecosystem from the ground up, and shifting an entire organization's relationship to knowledge as a result.

Role
Senior Knowledge Manager, owner
Scope
Global · Customer + Internal
Audience
Customers, Support, CS, Self-Service
Disciplines
Strategy · IA · Governance · KCS
01 / Context

The system before the system.

When I joined Flexera, knowledge existed, just not as a system. Articles lived across multiple repositories with different conventions. Ownership was diffuse. There was no agreed taxonomy, no defined lifecycle, no governance forum, and no shared standards for what a publishable article looked like. Authors were thoughtful but isolated; the platform was capable but underused.

The organizational cost of that wasn't just messy content. It was friction in every direction: customers couldn't find what they needed, Support agents resolved cases without capturing what they'd learned, Customer Success teams duplicated material instead of reusing it, and the knowledge base couldn't credibly anchor self-service or any future AI retrieval.

My remit was to turn that into operational infrastructure: governed, instrumented, scalable, and trusted by the teams who depend on it.

02 / Approach

Five structural moves.

I treated this as a system-design problem first, a content problem second. Adding more articles into a system without governance just makes the underlying mess louder. I focused on five interlocking moves: governance, IA, KCS, contributor enablement, and AI workflows, designed to reinforce each other.

  1. Established governance as a first-class function.

    Defined foundational policies, workflows, and decision rights for content creation, review, retirement, and ownership. Stood up the operational rhythms that keep a knowledge system honest over time: quality reviews, lifecycle audits, and exception handling. Without this, every other move would have eroded within a year.

  2. Built the information architecture.

    Designed the taxonomy, metadata model, and content types that govern how knowledge is structured, labeled, and surfaced. This is the substrate for search, navigation, AI retrieval, and cross-channel reuse. The IA is also what lets the contributor program scale without producing chaos: every author works inside a defined frame.

  3. Embedded KCS into everyday work.

    Translated KCS principles into the actual workflows of Support and Customer Success teams, capturing knowledge as a byproduct of resolving cases, not as a separate documentation task. Designed the recognition, enablement, and quality models that make KCS sustainable past launch enthusiasm.

  4. Grew a 200+ contributor community.

    Stood up a global contributor program with structured onboarding, role-based enablement, AI-assisted review and editing workflows, and tooling that makes participation low-friction. The shift was from “a small writing team produces all knowledge” to “the people closest to the work participate, supported by infrastructure.”

  5. Designed AI-assisted authoring into the system.

    Built an internal AI tool that drafts and edits knowledge articles from support cases using structured templates and style guidelines (case study 02). Because the IA, taxonomy, and style standards already existed, the AI could enforce them, producing consistent, on-brand articles instead of plausible-sounding noise.

03 / Outcomes

What changed.

The deepest outcome is structural: knowledge stopped being a side concern and became infrastructure that other parts of the business plan around. The clearest signal that the system was working showed up fast.

10K+

Knowledge articles governed across a 50+ product enterprise ecosystem.

4×

Growth in case-linked articles in a single year as knowledge moved into daily workflows.

200+

Global contributors writing and maintaining articles, across support and the wider solutions org.

Article contribution also more than doubled in a year as structured enablement, training, and recognition made knowledge creation part of the everyday workflow rather than a separate task.

The qualitative shift matters as much as the numbers: knowledge is now treated as operational infrastructure with named owners and a maintenance budget, AI-assisted authoring runs on top of governed structure rather than instead of it, and the contributor program creates a flywheel where the people closest to customers are also the people writing the articles.

04 / Reflections

What I'd tell another team.

Governance is the unglamorous lever. Everyone wants to talk about taxonomy and AI. But a knowledge system without governance (ownership, lifecycle, quality cadence) rots faster than you can rebuild it. Solve governance first; everything else compounds on top of it.

The contributor program is the strategy. A central writing team can't out-produce the operational reality of a global support and CS organization. The system has to make it easier for the people closest to the work to contribute well than to not contribute at all.

AI doesn't fix structure problems, it amplifies whatever structure you already have. Structured content, clean metadata, and explicit style standards are what make AI-assisted authoring trustworthy. Without them, AI just produces faster mediocrity.
