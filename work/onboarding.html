Case Study / 05 · 2023 — 2024

The product-agnostic onboarding program.

Designing Flexera's first onboarding program built to work for every new customer, regardless of which product they bought first.

Role
Designer & program owner
Built at
Flexera
Audience
All new Flexera customers
Disciplines
Onboarding · Instructional design · Self-service
01 / Context

Onboarding as a system, not an event.

Flexera's portfolio spans multiple products, each with its own onboarding pattern, its own pace, and its own assumptions about what the customer already knows. That made sense product by product, and made no sense at all when a customer was being onboarded for the first time and had to absorb four different versions of "welcome to Flexera."

The opportunity was to build a foundation layer: the things every new customer needs to know regardless of which product they started with. A consistent welcome, a clear orientation to support and resources, the shared concepts that show up in every product, and a defined path into the product-specific onboarding that follows.

02 / Approach

Three deliverables, one experience.

I treated this as a content-system problem with an instructional-design surface. The deliverables had to feel like one program, even though they served different moments in the customer journey.

  1. A self-service onboarding video course.

    A short, modular video curriculum that any new customer can complete on their own time. It introduces Flexera as a company, the support and community resources available, and the conceptual foundations that recur across products. Self-service-first, scalable to any volume of new customers.

  2. A “Getting Started” content system.

    A structured set of written content, designed against the existing IA and style standards, covering the product-agnostic essentials. Authored once, surfaced consistently across the customer-facing knowledge ecosystem and the support portal, so the experience matches no matter where customers enter.

  3. A standardized onboarding process.

    The operational layer: defining who does what, when, and how onboarding hand-offs work between teams. A predictable rhythm replacing ad-hoc patterns that varied by product, customer, and CSM.

03 / Outcomes

What changed for customers.

The program created a consistent first impression for every new customer, replaced product-specific improvisation with a predictable shared foundation, and gave Customer Success teams a place to point people that doesn't require a meeting to consume.

04 / Reflections

What I'd carry forward.

Find the layer that's actually shared. The hard work in a multi-product onboarding system isn't writing the content, it's identifying what truly belongs at the foundation versus what only feels foundational because a single product team thinks of it that way.

Self-service isn't “less than” high-touch. A well-designed self-service course is more consistent, more scalable, and often more respectful of a customer's time than a series of meetings. The point isn't to remove the human; it's to free the human up for the conversations that actually need them.

Onboarding lives inside the knowledge system, not next to it. Treating the program as part of the broader knowledge ecosystem (same IA, same style, same lifecycle) meant it could be governed and improved with the same machinery as everything else, instead of becoming another thing to maintain.
