Information architecture as rhetoric.

A research argument that the way we structure, label, and reveal information is itself an argument about meaning, authority, and trust.

Type
Graduate research
Program
MA, Technical Communication — UCF
Status

01 / Abstract

The premise.

Information architecture is usually framed as a neutral practice: a matter of findability, retrieval, and cognitive load. This research argues that IA is also rhetorical: the way we structure, label, and reveal information is an argument about what counts, what relates to what, and where authority resides. Every taxonomy is a position. Every navigation choice foregrounds some meanings and obscures others. Every default is a claim.

Reframing IA as rhetoric has practical implications, especially as AI-driven retrieval reshapes how people encounter information at scale. If the structure of information is an argument, then the people who design that structure are doing rhetorical work, and the discipline can no longer treat itself as merely technical.

02 / Outline

The shape of the argument.

Editor's note (visible only to you): Replace the outline below with the real chapter / section structure of your piece as it firms up. The numbered list pattern matches the rest of the site and works well for an academic outline.

  1. The neutrality myth.

    How IA came to be framed as a neutral, user-centered, "findability" discipline, and what that framing makes invisible.

  2. Taxonomy as argument.

    Categorization as an act of meaning-making. What it means to choose one parent category over another, to label one concept in one team's vocabulary versus another's, to admit or exclude a term.

  3. Navigation, hierarchy, and authority.

    How structure encodes power: what is foregrounded, what is buried, who gets to decide, and what readers learn to expect from a system before they read a single article.

  4. The AI inflection.

    What changes when retrieval is mediated by models trained on existing structures. The rhetorical implications of letting an opaque system determine which arguments surface first.

  5. Practical implications.

    What this reframing means for practitioners: how to design IA with rhetorical intent, how to make implicit arguments explicit, and how to govern systems that are now, unavoidably, persuasive.
