Project / 2024

Restructuring a graduate handbook.

A full editorial revision of the University of Utah Handbook for Theses and Dissertations. Turning a dense, overly formal academic document into something clearer, more accessible, and easier to use.

Subject
University of Utah Graduate Handbook
Discipline
Editorial · IA · Content design
Audience
Graduate students
Status
Published · 2024
01 / Context

A thorough document that was hard to use.

The original University of Utah Graduate Handbook for Theses and Dissertations was thorough, but its format made it difficult for students to actually use. Modeled after a thesis itself, the document was dense, overly formal, and visually uninviting. Critical requirements were buried within long sections, leading to confusion and frustration.

After receiving repeated complaints from students about how difficult the handbook was to understand and follow, staff reached out for help improving its clarity and usability. The brief was simple: keep the institutional rigor, but make it work for the people who actually have to use it.

02 / Analysis

What I found.

Before changing anything, I read the full document carefully against the user goal: a graduate student trying to figure out what their thesis or dissertation actually requires. Six structural problems surfaced quickly.

  1. Overwhelming layout.

    Long paragraphs with few visual cues meant readers had to wade through walls of prose to find anything specific.

  2. Inconsistent formatting.

    Variable heading levels, list styles, and spacing made it hard to scan or build a mental model of the document’s structure.

  3. Contradictory or unclear information.

    Different sections gave different versions of the same requirement, or stated rules in language vague enough that students couldn’t apply them with confidence.

  4. Missing or vague examples.

    Most rules had no worked example, leaving students to guess what compliance actually looked like in practice.

  5. Formal, academic tone.

    The voice was distant and authoritative in a way that signaled institutional gravity but offered no warmth or guidance, an obstacle, not a partner.

  6. Disconnected visual style.

    The document didn’t reflect university branding, which made it feel less like an authoritative resource and more like an internal draft.

03 / Approach

What I changed.

I restructured the handbook to improve clarity, consistency, and usability while preserving academic rigor. The principle behind every move: a handbook is a working tool, not a thesis. It should be designed for how students actually use it: under deadline pressure, looking for a specific answer, often returning to the same passage multiple times.

  1. Streamlined structure with clear hierarchy.

    Replaced the chapter-based, thesis-mimicking structure with smaller, logically grouped sections aligned to student tasks and decision points. Requirements became easier to find, easier to apply, and easier to revisit.

  2. Consistent formatting and visual rhythm.

    Standardized headings, lists, spacing, and emphasis patterns so the document supports scanning and reads as a coherent system rather than a stitched-together draft.

  3. Resolved contradictions, clarified ambiguity.

    Reconciled conflicting statements, rewrote vague guidance into actionable rules, and ensured each requirement was stated once, in one place, with the right level of detail.

  4. Added specific examples in context.

    Embedded worked examples directly within each section so students could see how each rule applies, reducing the “am I doing this right?” uncertainty that drives most help-seeking.

  5. Refined the tone.

    Shifted the voice from formal-distant to supportive and instructional. The document still carries institutional authority, but now it sounds like a guide rather than a gatekeeper.

  6. Aligned visual style with university branding.

    Updated typography, color, and layout to match the University of Utah’s identity, reinforcing the document’s authority and making it feel like an official resource rather than an internal draft.

05 / Reflections

Turning complexity into clarity.

The handbook problem is the same problem every governance document has: it has to be authoritative and usable, and most of them solve only the first half. Authority comes through tone, comprehensiveness, and gravity. Usability comes through structure, clarity, and design. Treating those as the same job, or assuming that being thorough is enough, is what produces documents people complain about.

What I’d carry forward to any institutional content rewrite: structure tells the reader what kind of document they’re holding before they read a word. A thesis-shaped handbook says “this is a thesis, read it linearly.” A reference-shaped handbook says “this is a tool, find what you need.” The structural choice is the rhetorical choice.
